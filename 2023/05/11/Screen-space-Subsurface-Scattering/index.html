Subsurface Scattering

When a ray of light hits the surface of an object, it sometimes goes into the surface and comes out again after numeral times of scattering inside the surface. This phenomenon is called subsurface scattering, which is typical for materials such as skin, marble and wax and would cause the object to seem translucent.

In order to describe such a behavior, the BSSRDF model was introduced. The model comes as a replacement of the original BRDF model, which describes the outgoing radiance as an integral of incoming radiance at the intersection point multiplied with a function representing the reflectance over the hemisphere determined by the local normal vector. Its successor takes subsurface scattering into account by changing the integral into one over the surface of the object and each hemisphere determined by the integrated point on the surface and the corresponding local normal vector. \[ L_o=\int_A\int_{2\pi}S(x_i,\vec\omega_i;x_o,\vec\omega_o)L_i(x_i,\vec\omega_i)|\cos\theta_i|d\vec\omega_idA \] The \(S\) in the equation above is the so-called BSSRDF(Bidirectional Scattering Surface Reflectance Distribution Function). It can furthermore be written as: \[ S(x_i,\vec\omega_i;x_o,\vec\omega_o)=S_d(x_i,\vec\omega_i;x_o,\vec\omega_o)+S^{(1)}(x_i,\vec\omega_i;x_o,\vec\omega_o) \] The equation above means that the light can leave the surface after either many times of scattering or only one. The first term on the right-hand-side is the diffusion(multi-scattering) term, which is less sensitive to the incoming and outgoing direction than the single-scattering term on its right.

The diffusion term can be expanded again as follows, since the light goes through two Fresnel refractions before and after the diffusion. \[ S_d(x_i,\vec\omega_i;x_o,\vec\omega_o)=\frac1{\pi}F_t(\eta,\vec\omega_i)R_d(||x_i-x_o||)F_t(\eta,\vec\omega_o) \] The \(R_d\) is the diffusion profile, it determines how the radiance attenuates after travelling length \(||x_i-x_o||\). It might be worth mentioning that I did read about someone questioning the necessity of the two Fresnel terms here, but for now I'm going with them.

A commonly used diffusion profile called Dipole is derived from the dipole model. I wish I could elaborate but it has been ages since I touched physics(and my friend working on nuclear physics would get mad at me for saying bullshit), so I'll just give you the equation here. \[ R(\rho,z_0)=\frac{1}{4\pi}[z_o(\mu_{eff}+\frac{1}{r_1})\frac{e^{-\mu_{eff}r_1}}{r_1^2}+(z_0+2z_b)(\mu_{eff}+\frac{1}{r_2})\frac{e^{-\mu_{eff}r_2}}{r_2^2}] \] The Dipole model only takes into consideration the multiple scattering part, so the single-scattering term has to be calculated or approximated using other methods.

Another diffusion profile derives from fitting a Monte-Carlo simulation of light going straight into a surface. The model is called Normalized BSSRDF. \[ R_d(r)=A\frac{e^{-r/d}+e^{-r/3d}}{8\pi dr} \] The change made by BSSRDF upon BRDF means that the outgoing radiance is contributed by the incoming radiance at each and every patch of the surface, although some patches have little weight.

A Naïve Attempt

One simple way to calculate subsurface scattering is to directly discretize the BSSRDF equation. \[ L_o\approx\frac{A}{N}\sum_{k=1}^NS(x_k;x_o)\int_{2\pi}L_i(x_k,\vec\omega_i)|\cos\theta_i|d\vec\omega_i \] Like described in previous introduction, the multiple-scattering component is not very sensitive to the incoming and outgoing direction of the ray, so we can presume that the ray goes perpendicular to the surface. The surface integral becomes the average of irradiance at each point multiplied by a weight describing how much the point contributes to the outgoing point.

This method involves the sum of samples on the surface, and luckily, the easiest way for us to get such a set of samples is to use the vertices of a triangular mesh. I came up with this "method" for a comparison experiment in a lab research. You can use either Dipole or Normalized BSSRDF for the diffuse profile, but the latter suffers from artifact brought by its divergence near \(r=0\).

So things become clear now:

  1. We collect incoming radiance at each vertex using a compute shader or path tracing.
  2. We perform an \(O(N^2)\) algorithm, iterating over all vertices to compute the outgoing radiance at each vertex.
  3. We use the stored outgoing radiance to shade all fragments, like in the vanilla vertex-fragment shader routine.

Screen-space Subsurface Scattering

SSSSS(Screen-space Subsurface Scattering) follows the idea that the whole subsurface scattering stuff can be approximated as a blurring done to the original diffuse component, which, in prior implementations, were done in texture space or by pre-integrating.

The logic here sounds very similar to that of Deferred Shading, which is a technique to avoid useless calculation for shading fragments that do not pass depth test(or occluded by opaque fragments in the front of them, in other words). In depth shading we store positions, normal vectors and other data into the G-buffer and shade only fragments that pass depth test. Deferred shading has some drawbacks such as being unable to blend or do SMAA, but the demo here does not really need such features, so let's just go with deferred shading.

So as a start, let's build a deferred shading pipeline. The basic idea of the pipeline involves two passes:

  1. Render the object and store geometry data into the G-buffer as textures.
  2. Render a quad and retrieve geometry data for the current pixel from the G-buffer and do the same old shading thing.

I used a 4-channel texture to store the position, with the last channel containing a 0-1 value indicating whether the pixel is on the bunny or not.
